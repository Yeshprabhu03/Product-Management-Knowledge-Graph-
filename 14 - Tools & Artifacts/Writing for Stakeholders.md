# Writing for Stakeholders

## Definition
Writing for Stakeholders is the art of creating asynchronous documents (emails, Slack updates, PRDs, memos) that are optimized for clarity, brevity, and action. Unlike "Creative Writing," stakeholder writing is designed to be "scannable" and "high-signal." It involves understanding the "Mental Model" of the reader and providing them with exactly the information they need to make a decision or provide feedback, while minimizing cognitive load.

## Why It Matters for PMs
For a Product Manager, writing is your "Primary Scalability Tool." It matters because:
1. **Saves Time**: A well-written 1-page memo can replace a 60-minute meeting with 10 people, saving the company hundreds of dollars in "Meeting Debt."
2. **Creates a "Paper Trail"**: Providing a permanent, searchable record of decisions, rationale, and scope that prevents future "He-said, She-said" conflicts.
3. **Increases Decision Velocity**: Allowing busy executives to review and approve your plans "offline" at their own convenience.

## How to Apply It
1. **The "BLUF" Method (Bottom Line Up Front)**: Put the most important information (the decision or the request) in the very first sentence. Don't make the reader "hunt" for the point.
2. **Use Clear Hierarchy**: Use bold headers, bullet points, and numbered lists to make the document scannable. Avoid "walls of text."
3. **Tailor the "Detail Density"**: Give an executive a 3-bullet summary; give an engineer a 5-page technical spec. Don't send the same document to everyone.
4. **Include a "Request for Action"**: Clearly state what you want the reader to do (e.g., "Approve the roadmap by Friday" or "Provide feedback on the UX").
5. **Edit for Brevity**: Before sending, challenge yourself to cut 20% of the words. If a sentence doesn't add value to the decision, delete it.

## Real-World Example
> **Amazon's "6-Page Memos" — 2000s to Present**
> Jeff Bezos famously banned PowerPoint at Amazon in favor of "6-page narrative memos." Before any decision is made, the team sits in silence for 20 minutes reading the memo. This forces the PM to **write with extreme clarity** and logic. Because there are no "bullets" to hide behind, the PM must explain the "why" and the "how" in a complete narrative, leading to much deeper discussions and better long-term decisions.
> *PM Lesson: Writing is thinking; if you can't write a clear 1-page memo on your feature, you don't understand it well enough to build it.*

## Common Pitfalls
- **"Reply-All" Bloat**: Sending massive updates to people who don't need them, leading to "Inbox Fatigue" and people ignoring your future messages.
- **Passive Voice and Weasel Words**: Using language like "it is believed that..." or "we might..." which hides accountability and creates ambiguity.
- **Ignoring the "Channel"**: Using Slack for a complex 3-month roadmap update (where it gets lost) or using a formal 10-page doc for a quick "yes/no" question.
