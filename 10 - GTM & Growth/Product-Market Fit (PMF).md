# Product-Market Fit (PMF)

## Definition
Product-Market Fit (PMF) is the stage where a product has successfully identified a target customer segment and is providing a value proposition that satisfies a significant market demand. It is the point where "the market pulls the product out of the startup" (Marc Andreessen). PMF is not a binary state but a spectrum, often measured quantitatively through the **Sean Ellis Survey** (asking users how disappointed they would be if the product disappeared) and high retention rates, and qualitatively through "Word of Mouth" and organic growth.

## Why It Matters for PMs
For a Product Manager, reaching PMF is the primary goal of the "Discovery" phase. It matters because:
1. **Unlocks Scalability**: Scaling a product before you have PMF is the most common cause of startup failure (premature scaling).
2. **Focuses Resources**: Allowing the team to stop "pivoting" and start "optimizing" the core value proposition for a specific segment.
3. **Validates the Vision**: Proving that the product's long-term goal actually resonates with real-world users and their willingness to pay.

## How to Apply It
1. **Run the PMF Survey**: Ask your active users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use [Product]?" If >40% say "Very Disappointed," you are likely nearing PMF.
2. **Analyze Retention Cohorts**: Look for a "flattening" retention curve. If users stop churning after a certain period, you have found a sustainable segment.
3. **Identify your "High Expectation Customers" (HXC)**: Find the specific users who love your product the most and identify what they have in common.
4. **Double-Down on the Wedge**: Once you find a small segment with high PMF, focus all your marketing and product efforts on dominating that "wedge" before expanding.
5. **Monitor for "Market Drift"**: Realize that PMF can be lost if the market moves, a new competitor emerges, or your product quality degrades over time.

## Real-World Example
> **Superhuman — 2017**
> Superhuman famously used the PMF survey to navigate their early growth. They found that while their overall "Very Disappointed" score was only 22%, it was over 50% for "founders and managers." They decided to ignore the feedback from other segments and focus exclusively on the needs of founders. This "segment-first" approach allowed them to reach a 58% PMF score within a few months, creating one of the most successful high-end productivity tools.
> *PM Lesson: PMF is often found in a subset of your users; finding that subset is as important as building the product itself.*

## Common Pitfalls
- **Scaling Too Early**: Investing heavily in marketing and sales before you have a stable, high-retention product, which leads to high churn and wasted capital.
- **Ignoring the Churn**: Thinking you have PMF because your "New Users" count is growing, while ignoring the fact that your "Total Active Users" is flat.
- **Vague Market Definition**: Trying to find PMF for "everyone," which leads to a diluted value proposition that nobody truly loves.
