# 🎯 OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

## 📖 Definition
OKRs are a collaborative goal-setting framework used by teams to set ambitious, measurable goals with trackable outcomes. An **Objective** is a qualitative, aspirational statement of what you want to achieve. **Key Results** are 3-5 quantitative metrics that measure progress toward that objective. Unlike traditional KPIs, OKRs are designed to be "stretch goals" that encourage teams to think beyond incremental improvements and focus on high-impact outcomes.

## 🎯 Why It Matters for PMs
For Product Managers, OKRs are essential for:
1. **Strategic Alignment**: Ensuring the team's work is directly connected to the company's high-level Product Vision and Strategy.
2. **Outcome Focus**: Moving the conversation away from "what we are building" (outputs) to "what impact we are making" (outcomes).
3. **Autonomy and Clarity**: Providing the team with a clear goal while allowing them the freedom to discover the best tactical way to achieve it.

## 🏗️ How to Apply It
1. **Set Ambitious Objectives**: Write 2-3 qualitative goals for the quarter that are inspiring and clearly aligned with the Product Strategy.
2. **Define Measurable Key Results**: For each objective, identify 3-5 metrics that would prove the objective has been met. Ensure they are outcomes (e.g., "Increase retention by 10%") not outputs (e.g., "Launch feature X").
3. **Cascade and Negotiate**: Discuss the OKRs with both leadership and the execution team to ensure they are realistic yet challenging.
4. **Track and Review Regularly**: Check progress on KRs every 2-4 weeks to identify blockers and adjust tactics if the current path isn't moving the needle.
5. **Score and Reflect**: At the end of the quarter, score the OKRs (usually on a 0.0 to 1.0 scale) and conduct a post-mortem to learn for the next cycle.

## 📦 Real-World Example
> **Google — 1999 to Present**
> Google popularized OKRs to drive its massive growth. One famous objective was "Organize the world's information." A specific Key Result was to "Index 100% of the web's accessible pages." This KR was purely outcome-based; it didn't specify *how* to build the crawler or the index, but it gave the engineering team a massive, measurable challenge that forced them to innovate on infrastructure before they could even focus on search quality.
> *PM Lesson: Outcome-based KRs provide the necessary pressure for technical innovation while leaving the specific "how" to the experts.*

## ⚠️ Common Pitfalls
- **Output-Based KRs**: Setting "Ship feature X" as a KR. If you ship the feature but it doesn't move any business metrics, you haven't actually succeeded.
- **Too Many OKRs**: Trying to track more than 3 objectives per team leads to diluted focus and prevents deep work on the most important goals.
- **Lack of Executive Support**: OKRs fail if leadership continues to manage the team based on a fixed feature roadmap rather than the agreed-upon outcomes.
