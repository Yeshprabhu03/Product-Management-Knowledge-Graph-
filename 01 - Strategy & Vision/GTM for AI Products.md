# 🚀 GTM for AI Products

## 📖 Definition
Go-To-Market (GTM) for AI Products is the specialized strategy for launching and scaling features powered by machine learning. Unlike traditional software GTM, it must manage the "Hype-Reality Gap," establish user trust before the model is fully mature, and often includes a beta strategy where early users serve as model trainers. The core shift is from promoting "Technology" (we use AI) to promoting "Outcomes" (we solve X problem faster), while simultaneously managing expectations around the probabilistic nature of AI outputs.

## 🎯 Why It Matters for PMs
For an AI PM, the GTM strategy is critical because:
1. **Manages Expectations**: Prevents the backlash that occurs when a hyped AI feature underperforms in real-world scenarios.
2. **Builds the Data Flywheel**: Ensures the initial launch captures enough high-quality data to improve the model for the broader market.
3. **Calibrates Trust**: Helps users understand when to rely on the AI and when to verify its outputs, protecting the product's reputation.

## 🏗️ How to Apply It
1. **Focus on Outcomes, Not Models**: Frame the product's value in terms of time saved or quality improved, rather than the name of the LLM or model architecture used.
2. **Identify "Early Adopter" Beachheads**: Target segments that are more tolerant of model errors in exchange for the 10x value the AI provides.
3. **Implement a Feedback Loop**: Ensure the UI makes it effortless for early users to flag errors, which then feeds back into model retraining.
4. **Plan for Fallbacks**: Have a clear story for what happens when the AI is down or uncertain, so the user never hits a "dead end."
5. **Communicate Transparency**: Be honest about the limitations and data usage of the AI to build long-term enterprise and consumer trust.

## 📦 Real-World Example
> **Notion AI — 2023**
> Notion AI's GTM strategy was a masterclass in outcome-focused positioning. Their waitlist landing page didn't lead with "GPT-4" or technical jargon. Instead, it showed three clear outcomes: "Write faster," "Think better," and "Summarize instantly." By focusing on the utility within the existing workspace, they drove over 1 million waitlist signups before the product even launched, proving demand without over-committing to model quality.
> *PM Lesson: In a crowded AI market, the product that positions itself as the best solution to a specific workflow will win over the one that positions itself as the best technology.*

## ⚠️ Common Pitfalls
- **Leading with Technology**: Thinking that "Powered by AI" is still a sufficient value proposition. It is now a commodity; the outcome is what matters.
- **Overpromising Accuracy**: Claiming 100% accuracy for a probabilistic system, which leads to immediate user frustration and loss of trust.
- **Ignoring the Beta Period**: Launching to everyone at once before the model has been "battle-tested" with diverse real-world data from a smaller, controlled group.
