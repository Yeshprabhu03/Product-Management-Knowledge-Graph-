# Product Strategy

## Definition
Product Strategy is the high-level plan that describes how a product will achieve its vision while competing effectively in the market. It involves making deliberate choices about market entry, differentiation, and the sequence of features or segments to tackle. Strategy is about focus — deciding what NOT to do is just as important as deciding what to do. It bridges the gap between the aspirational Product Vision and the tactical Product Roadmap.

## Why It Matters for PMs
Strategy is the PM's "Why." It matters because:
1. **Focuses Resources**: Directs the team's limited time and energy toward the most impactful opportunities.
2. **Differentiates**: Ensures the product offers a unique value proposition that competitors cannot easily replicate.
3. **Sequences Growth**: Helps in planning the progression from a niche solution to a broad platform.

## How to Apply It
1. **Analyze the Market**: Understand the current competitive landscape, user pain points, and emerging technology trends.
2. **Identify the Lever**: Find the one or two areas where your product can offer 10x more value than the current status quo.
3. **Define Your Moat**: Determine how you will protect your position — whether through data network effects, switching costs, or proprietary technology.
4. **Sequence Your Roadmap**: Plan your market entry (e.g., starting with a specific vertical) and outline the logical expansion paths for the next 12-24 months.
5. **Communicate the Choices**: Clearly articulate the trade-offs you are making so the entire organization understands the strategic direction.

## Real-World Example
> **Notion — 2016**
> Notion started with a vertical strategy, focusing on being a better "Note-taking and Wiki" tool for individuals. However, their broader strategy was to become a horizontal "All-in-one Workspace." They sequenced their growth by adding databases, then project management, then AI, gradually replacing multiple point solutions (Evernote, Trello, Confluence) within a single integrated platform.
> *PM Lesson: A successful strategy starts with a narrow, winning wedge before expanding into broader, more complex horizontal territories.*

## Common Pitfalls
- **Confusing Features with Strategy**: A list of features is not a strategy; strategy is the logic that explains why those features will win.
- **Lack of Differentiation**: Building a "me-too" product with no clear reason for users to switch from an established competitor.
- **Static Strategy**: Failing to adjust the plan as the market changes or as new data about user behavior emerges.
