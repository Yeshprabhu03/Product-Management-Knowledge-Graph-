# Business Models

## Definition
A Business Model describes the rationale of how an organization creates, delivers, and captures value. In product management, it specifically refers to the revenue streams, pricing structures, and unit economics that sustain the product. Common models include Subscription (SaaS), Usage-based, Freemium, Marketplace, and Platform. The core health of a business model is often measured by the LTV:CAC ratio (Lifetime Value to Customer Acquisition Cost), which determines the long-term viability of the product.

## Why It Matters for PMs
Product Managers must understand the business model because:
1. **Informs Feature Prioritization**: Certain features may drive retention (LTV) while others drive acquisition (CAC).
2. **Aligns Product with Economics**: Ensures that the cost of building and running a feature (e.g., AI inference costs) does not exceed the revenue it generates.
3. **Dictates GTM Strategy**: The way you charge for a product significantly influences who buys it and how they use it.

## How to Apply It
1. **Identify Value Exchange**: Determine exactly what the user is paying for and what they get in return (e.g., access, efficiency, or networking).
2. **Select Revenue Streams**: Choose the primary way the product will make money (e.g., monthly subscription vs. a fee per transaction).
3. **Analyze Unit Economics**: Calculate the cost to serve one user (including server costs, support, and sales) and ensure it allows for a healthy margin.
4. **Test and Iterate on Pricing**: Regularly experiment with different price points and bundles to find the optimal balance between volume and profit.
5. **Align with User Behavior**: Ensure the way you charge (e.g., usage-based) matches how users naturally derive value from the product.

## Real-World Example
> **OpenAI — 2023**
> OpenAI employs a dual business model to capture value from different segments. For developers, they use a **Usage-based Model**, charging per token used in their API. This aligns their revenue directly with the success of their customers' apps. Simultaneously, for individual consumers, they use a **Subscription Model** (ChatGPT Plus), offering a flat monthly fee for premium access. This hybrid approach allows them to capture both high-volume developer usage and steady consumer recurring revenue.
> *PM Lesson: Different customer segments often require different business models to maximize value capture from the same underlying technology.*

## Common Pitfalls
- **Ignoring the Cost-to-Serve**: Especially in AI, failing to account for high inference costs can lead to a product that loses money on every new user.
- **Mismatched Incentives**: Using a model that encourages behavior contrary to the product's value (e.g., charging per user for a collaboration tool, which discourages team adoption).
- **Over-complicating Pricing**: Creating so many tiers and add-ons that potential customers become paralyzed by choice and fail to convert.
