# Career Transitions

## Definition
Career Transitions in PM refers to the deliberate moves between companies, industries, PM specializations, or from adjacent roles (engineering, design, consulting, data science) into product management. Each transition type carries different risk, different narrative requirements, and different success rates depending on how much transferable signal you can demonstrate.

## Why It Matters for PMs
1. **Compensation Reset**: Transitions are the fastest mechanism for significant comp increases — internal promotions average 5-15% raises; external moves average 20-40%.
2. **Specialization Pivots**: Moving from Core PM to AI PM, or from Consumer to Enterprise, requires deliberate repositioning — it rarely happens passively.
3. **Breaking Level Ceilings**: Some companies have flat PM hierarchies that cap progression. External moves are sometimes the only path to the next level.

## How to Apply It
1. **Define the Transition Type**: Lateral (same level, new company), Upward (next level), Specialization Pivot (new archetype), or Into PM (from adjacent role). Each requires a different preparation strategy.
2. **Build the Bridge Narrative**: Articulate why your past experience is additive, not a liability. A data scientist moving into AI PM should lead with model intuition, not apologize for lacking PRD experience.
3. **Target the Right Entry Point**: Moving into AI PM is easiest at companies expanding their AI teams (growth-stage, high urgency to hire) rather than established AI companies with high bars for prior experience.
4. **Time the Move**: The best time to move is 12-18 months after a major launch — you have a concrete outcome to discuss and references who can speak to shipped work.
5. **Build Before You Leave**: Start the transition 6 months before you want to move — build the relevant skill, write about it, and develop recruiter relationships in the target domain.

## Real-World Example
> **Ex-McKinsey to PM — common pattern**
> The consulting-to-PM transition is one of the most common and most mishandled. Consultants typically have strong strategic and stakeholder communication skills but no shipped product experience. The PMs who successfully made this transition (e.g., early Airbnb, Stripe, and Figma PMs from consulting backgrounds) focused their transition narrative on one thing: a side project, an internal tool, or an APM program that demonstrated they had shipped something and made trade-off decisions under constraint. The ones who failed led with strategic frameworks rather than execution evidence.
> *PM Lesson: In a transition, your new skill matters less than your ability to demonstrate that you have already started using it.*

## Common Pitfalls
- **Waiting for Permission**: Specialization transitions rarely happen through internal moves alone — most AI PM roles at AI companies require targeted external positioning.
- **Under-Leveling to Get In**: Taking a step down in level to break into a new domain can work, but sets a new comp baseline that is hard to recover from quickly.
- **Ignoring Network Leverage**: 60-70% of PM roles are filled through referrals — cold applications at senior levels have very low conversion rates regardless of resume quality.
