# Salary and Compensation

## Definition
PM compensation consists of four components: base salary, annual bonus (target %), equity (RSUs or options with a vesting schedule), and benefits/perks. Total Compensation (TC) is the standard metric used to compare offers across companies. Understanding how each component works — and what is negotiable — is a core professional skill that directly determines the financial return on your career investment.

## Why It Matters for PMs
1. **Compounding Effect**: Accepting below-market compensation creates a baseline that follows you — future offers are often anchored to your current salary, and the gap compounds over time.
2. **Equity Literacy**: Misunderstanding RSU vesting schedules, cliff periods, and tax treatment has cost PMs hundreds of thousands of dollars in poorly timed decisions.
3. **Negotiation is Expected**: Companies build negotiation margin into initial offers — not negotiating leaves real money on the table with no downside risk.

## How to Apply It
1. **Know Your Market Rate**: Use Levels.fyi (for tech), LinkedIn Salary, and direct peer conversations to establish TC ranges for your level and location before any negotiation.
2. **Evaluate Total Compensation**: Never compare base salaries. Model out 4-year TC including equity vesting, bonus probability, and refresh grants.
3. **Negotiate the Full Package**: If base is capped, negotiate signing bonus, accelerated vesting, equity refresh cadence, or title — all affect TC or future leverage.
4. **Use Competing Offers**: A real competing offer is the most effective negotiation tool. Even an offer you would not accept can be used to establish your market price.
5. **Understand Equity Risk**: Public company RSUs are liquid; private company options carry illiquidity risk and a strike price that may exceed the stock value at exit.

## Real-World Example
> **Meta PM — 2022**
> A senior PM received an initial offer of $220k base + $400k RSUs (4-year vest) + 15% bonus target. By presenting a competing offer from Google at comparable TC, they negotiated to $235k base + $550k RSUs + a $75k signing bonus — a $195k improvement in 4-year TC. The negotiation took two emails and three days. The company had allocated $600k in RSUs for the role; the initial offer was $400k.
> *PM Lesson: The initial offer is the company's preferred outcome, not the ceiling. Negotiating once, politely and with data, is almost always risk-free.*

## Common Pitfalls
- **Accepting the First Offer**: Most companies expect negotiation and will not rescind an offer for a reasonable counter.
- **Ignoring Equity Dilution**: At pre-IPO companies, the percentage of the company matters more than the number of shares or dollar value at a speculative valuation.
- **Anchoring on Base**: A $20k base increase at a large tech company is often less valuable than a $100k increase in RSU grant with similar base, due to tax treatment and vesting mechanics.
