#Generative AI Product Strategy

##Definition
A strategic framework for integrating Large Language Models (LLMs) and other generative technologies into a product portfolio. It balances "Innovation" (new capabilities) with "Distribution" (existing reach).

##Why It Matters for PMs
Generative AI is a commodity technology. The strategy is not about building the "Best Model," but about finding the "Best Application" where your existing data, distribution, or user trust creates a defensible moat that a generic AI cannot bridge.

##How to Apply It
1. Identify High-Value Use Cases: Focus on tasks that are "High Frequency, Low Risk" for AI assistance.
2. Evaluate "Make vs. Buy": Decide if you should use an API (OpenAI) or build a custom, fine-tuned model.
3. Leverage Distribution: Embed AI where users already work to lower the barrier to adoption.
4. Build Data Moats: Use user feedback and proprietary data to make your AI better than generic alternatives.

##Real-World Example
> **Microsoft + OpenAI — 2023**
> Microsoft's strategy was not to build an LLM — it was to embed one into every product in its existing $200B portfolio (Word, Excel, Teams, GitHub). The bet: distribution beats model quality. Copilot launched in 365 before Google Workspace AI, despite Google having a comparably capable model. First-mover advantage in distribution, not in AI, was the strategic insight.
> *PM Lesson: In AI, the question is rarely "build the best model." It's "who has the distribution to make a good-enough model win?"*

##Common Pitfalls
- Chasing "AI Hype" for features that don't solve a real user pain point.
- Underestimating the "Cost-to-Serve" (GPU costs) for generative features.
- Failing to differentiate from the base model's native capabilities (e.g., "ChatGPT can already do this").
