# Kano Model

## Definition
The Kano Model is a theory for product development and customer satisfaction which classifies customer preferences into five categories: Must-be, One-dimensional, Attractive, Indifferent, and Rejection. It helps PMs understand which features will truly delight users versus those that are simply expected.

## Why It Matters for PMs
It prevents PMs from over-investing in "Basics" (which don't increase satisfaction) and identifies "Delighters" that can create a competitive advantage. It also tracks how features migrate from "Delighters" to "Basics" over time.

## How to Apply It
1. Categorize Features: Use user surveys to classify features into Kano categories.
2. Prioritize the Mix: A healthy roadmap needs all Must-be's, some One-dimensional (to compete on performance), and at least one Attractive (to differentiate).
3. Monitor Decay: Regularly re-evaluate features to see if yesterday's delighter is today's expectation.

## Real-World Example
> **Apple — 2007**
> Push notifications were a "Delighter" (Attractive) in 2007 iPhone launch. Users didn't expect them and were thrilled. By 2012, every app had them and users expected them by default — they had migrated to "Must-be." Apple's PM insight was to recognize this shift and invest in notification management (Do Not Disturb) before competitors did.
> *PM Lesson: Delighters decay into Must-be's. Continuously discover what the next Delighter is before your competitors do.*

## Common Pitfalls
- Assuming that more features always lead to more satisfaction.
- Ignoring "Must-be" features because they aren't exciting to build.
- Failing to realize that Kano categories vary across different user segments.
