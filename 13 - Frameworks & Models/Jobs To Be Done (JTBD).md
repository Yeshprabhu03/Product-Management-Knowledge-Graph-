#Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

##Definition
A framework based on the theory that customers do not buy products or services; they "hire" them to perform a specific "job" — the progress an individual wants to make in a particular circumstance. A "Job" includes functional, emotional, and social dimensions.

##Why It Matters for PMs
Focusing on the "Job" rather than the "User" prevents PMs from building for narrow personas and allows them to identify non-obvious competitors. It forces a shift from "What features do they want?" to "What outcome are they trying to achieve?"

##How to Apply It
1. Define the Circumstance: Where, when, and with whom is the user when the need arises?
2. Identify the Three Dimensions:
    - Functional: The task to be completed.
    - Emotional: How the user wants to feel.
    - Social: How the user wants to be perceived.
3. Write Job Stories: "When [Situation], I want to [Motivation], so that I can [Desired Outcome]."
4. Evaluate Solutions: Does your AI feature help them do the job faster, better, or cheaper than the current "hire"?

##Real-World Example
> **Turntable.fm — 2011**
> The creators of the social music app Turntable.fm realized that the "Functional Job" was simply listening to music. However, the real "Social/Emotional Job" was to feel connected to others and gain status as a "tastemaker" while discovering new songs. By building live DJ rooms where users could vote and gain points, they solved the social job in a way that Spotify (at the time) did not.
> *PM Lesson: Understanding the social and emotional "Job" is often the difference between a utility and a habit-forming product.*

##Common Pitfalls
- Defining the "Job" too broadly (e.g., "be happy").
- Confusing a "Job" with a "Feature" (e.g., "The job is to have a dashboard").
- Ignoring the "Circumstance" (a user hires a milkshake for a different job in the morning than in the evening).
