# First Principles Thinking

## Definition
A problem-solving approach that involves breaking down a complex situation into its most basic, fundamental truths (the "First Principles") and then reasoning up from there, rather than reasoning by analogy or following "industry best practices."

## Why It Matters for PMs
In AI, "Best Practices" change every six months. PMs who rely on analogy ("It's like Uber for X") will miss the revolutionary possibilities of new models. First principles thinking allows PMs to see past existing product categories and build solutions that were previously impossible.

## How to Apply It
1. Identify the Assumptions: List everything you "think" is true about the problem (e.g., "Software must have a GUI").
2. Break Down to Fundamentals: What are the irreducible truths? (e.g., "The user wants to get from A to B").
3. Reason Up: Given these truths and the new capabilities of AI, what is the most efficient way to solve the problem today?
4. Challenge "Why": Ask "Why" until you reach a physical or logical constraint, rather than a social or historical one.

## Real-World Example
> **SpaceX — 2002**
> Elon Musk used First Principles Thinking to start SpaceX. Instead of accepting the assumption that "Rockets are too expensive for private companies," he looked at the raw material costs (aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber). He found that the materials cost only about 2% of the retail price of a rocket. This led him to the conclusion that the high cost was due to manufacturing inefficiency and lack of reusability — leading to the development of the Falcon 9.
> *PM Lesson: If the "Raw Materials" of your product (e.g., compute and data) are cheap, but the product is expensive, there is a massive opportunity for a first-principles redesign.*

## Applied to PM
Instead of saying "We need an AI search bar because our competitors have one," a First Principles PM asks: "What is the user's fundamental goal?" If the goal is "to find the answer to a question," maybe the solution isn't a search bar at all, but a proactive notification or a conversational agent that answers before the user even types.

## Common Pitfalls
- Confusing "First Principles" with "I have a better opinion."
- Ignoring the "Cost of Change" (analogies exist because they are familiar to users).
- Over-thinking simple problems that don't require deep deconstruction.
