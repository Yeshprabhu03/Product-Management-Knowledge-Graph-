# 🗺️ Product Roadmap

## 📖 Definition
A Product Roadmap is a shared source of truth that outlines the vision, direction, priorities, and progress of a product over time. It is a strategic document that aligns the organization on "why" and "what" is being built. Modern roadmaps are typically **Outcome-based** (focusing on goals and themes like "improve retention") rather than **Feature-based** (a list of specific deliverables). They often use time horizons like "Now / Next / Later" to provide direction without over-committing to rigid delivery dates.

## 🎯 Why It Matters for PMs
For a Product Manager, the roadmap is the primary communication tool to:
1. **Align Stakeholders**: Ensuring everyone from sales to engineering understands the product's strategic priorities.
2. **Manage Expectations**: Providing a clear picture of what the team is focusing on and what is being deferred.
3. **Connect Tactics to Strategy**: Showing how every upcoming feature or theme directly contributes to the high-level Product Vision and OKRs.

## 🏗️ How to Apply It
1. **Define Your Strategic Themes**: Group your potential work into high-level outcomes (e.g., "Mobile Onboarding," "Enterprise Security").
2. **Prioritize the Themes**: Use frameworks like RICE or Value vs. Complexity to decide which themes deserve the most focus in the next quarter.
3. **Use Time Horizons**: Instead of specific dates, use "Now" (active work), "Next" (confirmed for the next cycle), and "Later" (vague goals for the future).
4. **Tailor for the Audience**: Create a detailed version for engineering and a simplified, goal-oriented version for sales and executives.
5. **Update and Prune Regularly**: Review the roadmap monthly to remove outdated ideas and reflect new learnings from user research and experiments.

## 📦 Real-World Example
> **Figma's Roadmap Philosophy — 2018**
> Figma moved away from a feature-based roadmap to an **Outcome-based Roadmap**. Instead of committing to "Add a new pen tool button," they committed to the theme of "Improving vector editing precision." This gave the engineering and design teams the flexibility to pivot their implementation based on user feedback during the quarter, while still fulfilling the strategic promise to stakeholders. It prevented "Roadmap Lock," where teams ship bad features just because they were on the list.
> *PM Lesson: A roadmap should be a commitment to solve a problem, not a commitment to build a specific solution.*

## ⚠️ Common Pitfalls
- **Feature-Only Roadmaps**: Treating the roadmap as a giant "To-Do" list, which stifles innovation and prevents the team from pivoting based on data.
- **Over-committing to Dates**: Giving specific delivery months for features that haven't been fully scoped, leading to "broken promises" and loss of trust.
- **Lack of Regular Pruning**: Letting the "Later" column become a "graveyard" of hundreds of ideas that the team will never actually build.
